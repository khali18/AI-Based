1.2 Problem Statement
Despite the digital transformation of hospital records, many pharmacy departments still face the "inventory paradox": the simultaneous occurrence of stockouts for essential drugs and the expiration of overstocked items. Research by Osei et al. (2024) indicates that without intelligent forecasting, procurement decisions are often based on static averages that do not account for seasonal disease trends or consumption velocity.
Current systems typically follow the First-Expired, First-Out (FEFO) method. While this ensures older stock is used first, it lacks the computational intelligence to predict if the current volume of a specific batch will be fully consumed before its expiration date. This leads to "expired-on-shelf" syndrome, where significant capital is lost because near-expiry drugs were not identified in time for redistribution. Without a predictive mechanism to correlate consumption rates with shelf life, hospitals continue to suffer from financial leakage and compromised patient care.

The study focuses on the development of the "Intelligence Layer" of a pharmacy inventory system. The primary limitation is the reliance on historical data; sudden, unpredictable events such as a pandemic may temporarily affect the accuracy of the predictive model. CHAPTER TWO: LITERATURE REVIEW
2.1 Introduction
The transition from manual inventory oversight to autonomous, data-driven systems represents a significant evolution in healthcare informatics. This chapter evaluates the theoretical frameworks and existing technological solutions surrounding pharmaceutical stock management. It examines the limitations inherent in current reactive models, explores the mathematical foundations of predictive analytics, and highlights the specific functional gaps that the proposed AI-Based Medicine Stock and Expiry Prediction System is designed to bridge.
2.2 Conceptual Review
2.2.1 Pharmaceutical Logistics and Inventory Control
Inventory control in a medical context is defined as the systematic process of regulating the receipt, storage, and distribution of drugs to ensure constant availability. Unlike general retail, pharmaceutical logistics are constrained by high sensitivity to time and the legal implications of dispensing expired products. According to Smith and Watkins (2023), effective control is achieved only when "stock velocity" is perfectly aligned with "procurement cycles," a balance that remains difficult to achieve through traditional means.
2.2.2 Machine Learning in Healthcare Operations
Machine Learning (ML) refers to the use of computational algorithms that improve automatically through experience. In clinical pharmacy, this involves training models on historical dispensing records to identify patterns that human operators might overlook. As noted by Miller (2025), the primary value of ML in this domain is its ability to convert "dormant data" into "actionable foresight," particularly in forecasting demand surges and identifying batches at risk of obsolescence.
2.3 Review of Existing Systems
2.3.1 Paper-Based and Legacy Electronic Records
Many healthcare facilities continue to rely on manual ledger books or basic spreadsheet applications. While these methods are low-cost, they are fundamentally "blind" to future trends. Osei et al. (2024) argue that manual systems create a "data silo" where information is recorded but never analyzed, leading to high rates of medicine expiration simply because the staff lacked a visual or automated warning mechanism.
2.3.2 Standard Pharmacy Information Systems (PIS)
Current-generation Pharmacy Information Systems (PIS) utilize barcodes and databases to track stock movements, often employing First-Expired, First-Out (FEFO) logic. However, these systems are "descriptive" rather than "predictive." They can report that a drug has expired, but they cannot calculate if a drug will expire based on the current speed of sales. This lack of "utilization-awareness" is a primary driver of pharmaceutical waste in modern hospitals.
2.4 Review of Related Technologies
2.4.1 Supervised Learning Algorithms
For the development of predictive layers, supervised learning is the standard approach.
Regression Analysis: Tools like Random Forest Regressors allow the system to process multiple variables—such as cost, category, and historical sales—to predict future inventory needs (Zhao et al., 2024).
Classification Engines: These are used to label stock batches with risk scores. By analyzing the "Time-to-Expiry" against "Consumption Rates," the engine can accurately categorize items as "High Risk" or "Stable."
2.4.2 Interactive Web Frameworks
Modern web technologies, specifically Python-based frameworks like Flask and FastAPI, provide the necessary infrastructure to serve AI models to end-users. Taylor (2025) emphasizes that the "User Experience" of an inventory system is just as important as the backend logic; using responsive dashboards with real-time alerts ensures that pharmacists can make split-second decisions without navigating complex data tables.
2.5 Review of Methods and Models
2.5.1 Demand Forecasting Methodologies
Predicting drug demand requires models that account for "seasonality" (the rise and fall of illnesses throughout the year). Literature suggests that Time-Series Analysis is superior to simple moving averages because it recognizes cyclic patterns in patient admissions and prescription rates (Gomez, 2023).
2.5.2 Expiry Risk Assessment Framework
The most effective model for expiry prediction is the Probability of Utilization model. This involves a mathematical comparison:
$$Risk = \frac{Current\_Stock}{Average\_Daily\_Sales} > Days\_to\_Expiry$$
If the predicted time to sell the stock is greater than the time remaining until expiry, the system identifies a "High Risk" event. This project adopts this logic to provide automated redistribution recommendations.
2.6 Gaps in Existing Systems
Critical analysis of current inventory solutions reveals three major deficiencies:
Passive Monitoring: Systems wait for a human to check a report rather than pushing proactive alerts.
Inflexible Reorder Points: Most systems use static "minimum levels" that do not adjust to changing disease trends.
Lack of Decision Support: Existing software identifies problems (e.g., "Medicine Expiring") but fails to provide AI-generated solutions (e.g., "Redistribute 50 units to the Maternity Ward").
2.7 Summary of Literature Review
The reviewed literature confirms that while digital tracking is common, "intelligent" inventory management is still in its infancy in many hospital settings. There is a verified need for a system that combines real-time stock tracking with predictive modeling. By filling the gaps in "utilization-awareness" and "proactive alerting," this project aims to provide a scalable solution that significantly reduces the financial and environmental burden of expired medications.
